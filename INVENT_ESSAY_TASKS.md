1. Evaluate the subject clarity of the cat in the photograph. Examine whether the cat is rendered with enough definition and crispness to register as the intentionally focused element. Look at the edges of the cat's form, the texture of its fur, the detail in its eyes and whiskers. Determine whether the cat feels present and tangible within the frame — as though the camera found it and held it — or whether it appears soft, ghostly, and approximate. This is not a judgment of absolute sharpness or technical resolution; it is a judgment of whether the cat, taken on its own terms, reads as a focused subject.

2. Evaluate the background separation between the cat and its surroundings. Examine whether the rest of the scene — everything that is not the cat — yields its claim on the viewer's attention by falling into softness, blur, or reduced definition relative to the cat. Determine whether the environment steps back to allow the cat to step forward, or whether it competes with the cat at a similar level of sharpness and detail. A scene where the background dissolves into soft shapes while the cat remains clear creates strong separation. A scene where every element is rendered with equal crispness offers the cat no special position in the visual hierarchy.

3. Evaluate the focal pull of the photograph toward the cat. Examine whether the image, taken as a whole, creates a sense of visual magnetism that draws the viewer's eye toward the cat and holds it there. Consider how the transition from sharp to soft radiates through the frame, whether the cat sits at the point of highest clarity and contrast, and whether the optical gradients in the image slope naturally toward the subject. Determine whether looking at the cat feels inevitable — as though the image leaves the viewer nowhere else to go — or whether the cat is simply one element among many that the viewer must actively search for.
